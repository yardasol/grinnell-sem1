Life cycle of Aedes (Stegomyia) aegypti (Diptera, Culicidae) in waters with different characteristics

 

Life cycle of Aedes (Stegomyia) aegypti (Diptera, Culicidae) in water with different characteristics

 

 

Eduardo B. BallI; Eraldo m. de Freitas(I); Jose t. de SouzaII; Carlos R. MIII; Keliana D. SantosIII

(I) Department of biology, State University of Paraíba, Ganesh Arruda Street s/n, Bodocongó, 58109-753 Campina Grande, PB, Brazil. (ebeserra@uol.com.br; ebarbosa@uepb.edu.br)

ABSTRACT

The present work aimed to estimate the effect of water quality in the development of Aedes aegypti (Linnaeus, 1762). The life cycle of this vector was studied in raw sewage water, effluent of UASB reactor, effluent of polishing lagoon, effluent of anaerobic filter, rainwater and de-chlorinated water. The development period, egg viability and larval and pupal survival were evaluated daily as well as the adult longevity and fecundity. The duration of larval period showed a variation of 5.6 to 9.1 days. Survival was considered low in raw sewage water, effluent of UASB reactor, effluent of polishing lagoon and effluent of anaerobic filter. No difference in terms of the duration and egg and pupal viability nor in the adult longevity and fecundity was detected. It was verified that despite a reduction in larval viability, A. aegypti could accomplish total development in waters with high pollution degree and that all the treatments were suitable to the development of this vector.

Keywords: Insecta, vector, dengue, biological cycle, water quality.

RESUMO

The study aimed to evaluate the effect of water quality in the development of Aedes aegypti (Linnaeus, 1762). The life cycle of this vector was studied in raw wastewater, effluent from UASB reactor, polishing pond effluent, effluent anaerobic filter, rain water and desclorada water. Daily evaluated the development period and the viabilities of egg, larva, pupa, longevity and fecundity of adults. The duration of the larval period ranged from 5.6 to 9.1 days, being the low survival in raw wastewater, effluent from UASB reactor, polishing pond effluent and effluent anaerobic filter. No differences were found in the durations and viabilities of the phases of egg and pupa, longevity and fecundity of adults. It was noted that, despite the low larval viability, it is possible the development of a. aegypti in waters with high degrees of pollution and that all treatments have enabled the development of this vector.

Keywords: Insecta, vector, dengue, biological cycle, water quality.

 

 

The Aedes aegypti (Linnaeus, 1762) is today one of the major problems in public health, due to his role as transmitter of dengue and yellow fever. Throughout its evolution, this culicídeo developed a strictly sinantrópico and anthropogenic behavior, being considered the mosquito species more dependent on urban environment (Christmas, 2002). Their habitat is closely connected to the home or peridomiciliares conditions offered by the way of life of human populations.

The process of urbanization without control is an important factor in the a. aegypti in cities. A large part of the populations of poor countries live in areas where basic sanitation service is deficient, resulting in accumulation of garbage in the peridomestic habitats and water storage in artificial containers, which serve as preferred breeding sites for this vector (TAUIL, 2002), a fact noted by SILVA et al. (2003) who recorded in Paraná, largest occurrence of a. aegypti in neighborhoods with large amount of garbage thrown by the population. According to GÓMEZ et al. (2001) the residences that are more likely to Harbor mosquito larvae are those with lower hygienic conditions.

The preference of the a. aegypti by artificial deposits causes the population concentration from with urbanization, next to the large use of artificial containers, is the determining factor in its increasing proliferation in urban centers of the tropical and subtropical regions of the planet. The choice of a location for oviposition is one of the main factors responsible for the distribution of mosquitoes in breeding grounds and their subsequent dispersal in different geographical areas (TILAK et al., 2004).

It is known that the a. aegypti presents a water cycle is influenced by the type and quality of water reservoirs. Second VAREJAO et al. (2005) this vector prefers reproduce in reservoirs of clean water, although it can adapt to new situations imposed by man, adapting to other types of breeding, like for example, bromeliads and open sewers found in various urban centres. This fact highlights the need to develop studies leading to the understanding of how environmental and human factors interfere with the establishment of the populations of a. aegypti, especially those who seek to understand the factors related to collections of water suitable for the development of this culicídeo. This study aimed to compare the life cycle of a. aegypti in waters with different degrees of pollution, rain water and desclorada water.

 

MATERIAL AND METHODS

Three bioassays were conducted to evaluate the effect of water quality in the development of a. aegypti. Laboratory bioassays and the creations of the populations of a. aegypti were conducted at the Center for integrated pest management at the State University of Paraíba, in creating room heated at 26 ± 2° C and a photophase of 12 hours, using the generation (F1) nauplii. The collections of eggs of a. aegypti occurred in the Nova Brasilia in Campina Grande, PB, where installed, every fifteen days, and until the establishment of populations in the laboratory, 50 traps for collecting eggs. Three days after its installation, the traps were collected and the collected material led to the lab for identification and establishment of creation.

Methodology of creation of Aedes aegypti. The creation of the larvae occurred in white plastic trays, covered by a fine mesh screen, where it was supplied as 100 mg of food ration for fish (Alcon/goldfish growth). The pupae were sexed and transferred to adult breeding cages (30 x 30 x 30 cm) who received 200 individuals, in the ratio of one male to every female. To adults was offered a 20% solution of honey and females allowed the blood meal in quail, Coturnix japonica (Temminck & Schlegel, 1849), for 30 minutes, three times a week. In each cage added a disposable Cup of 250 ml with desclorada water, containing within it a plastic funnel covered with filter paper to serve as a substrate for oviposition.

Life cycle of Aedes aegypti in different collections of water. Bioassays were repeated three times with the treatments: T1 = raw domestic sewage; T2 = anaerobic reactor effluent upward flow with sludge blanket (UASB), T3 = polishing pond effluent; T4 = effluent anaerobic filter and T5 = desclorada water. For the third bioassay, added to previous treatments a sixth treatment (T6), consisting of rain water. Four replicates were made, each consisting of five cups (250 ml) containing 10 larvae of first stage of a. aegypti and 200 ml of water, by adding at the beginning of the bioassays 1.9 mg/larva feed. The adults were kept in breeding cages constructed of wooden lathing telada (20 x 20 x 20 cm), each containing ten couples and repeated in five times. Have been assessed, the period of embryonic development, from the first 20 positions, the viability and duration of larval and pupal phase, the duration of the cycle of egg to adult emergence and fecundity of adults.

Physical and chemical analysis of the water. Were analyzed the following physical and chemical parameters as APHA (1998): ammoniacal nitrogen (N-NH4+), total Nitrogen (NTK), Total phosphate, Total alkalinity, chemical oxygen demand (D.Q.), total solids, biochemical oxygen demand (BOD5), turbidity, temperature and pH. The characteristics evaluated in each type of water were subjected to analysis of variance and averages compared by Tukey test (F) and the 5% probability.

 

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Average temperatures of the waters in the development of egg to adult emergence were between 24° C and 25° c. The collections of water during larval development presented a pH ranging from 6.91 to 8.15, 7.33 to 8.76 and 5.76 to 7.94 for bioassays, I, II and III respectively, being considered as neutral to slightly alkaline, with the exception of the third the bioassay analysis III, at the end of the larval development where raw sewage was 5.78 and pH for the water ballast UASB effluent pH was 5.76 therefore slightly acid. Were detected high concentrations of dissolved substances as total solids, total nitrogen, ammoniacal nitrogen and total phosphate and low concentrations of dissolved oxygen when compared to what was observed in desclorada water and rain water (Tabs. I-III).

The first bio-assay detected differences between the types of statistics collections of water, with respect to the duration and the viability of the larval stage, but were not detected significant differences regarding duration and viability of the pupal stage and the sex ratio (table IV). The only complete development II bioassay of insect larva, the emergence of adults in desclorada water, pond water and polishing of anaerobic filter (tab. V).

For the third bioassay were some physical and chemical parameters such as turbidity and conductivity not assessed in previous bioassays. There were significant differences regarding duration and viability of the larval period (table VI). As for the longevity of adults and number of eggs per female, there was no significant difference (tab. VII).

Regardless of the type of water and the bioassay considered, concerning periods of developments of the phases of egg, larva and pupa are within the range of standard time observed for a. aegypti and are according to the results of BALL et al. (2006), to studying the biology of five populations of a. aegypti, collected in the municipalities of Campina Grande, Boqueirão, Ramil, Brejo dos Santos and Itaporanga, Paraíba, the 26° C a time of development stages of egg, larva and pupa that ranged from 4.1 to 4.7 days respectively, 6.6 to 10.2 days and 2.1 to 2.7 days. Next to these values were also observed by QUIET & NAVARRO-SILVA (2002) that in similar conditions in thermal requirements study with Aedes albopictus Skuse, 1894, observed a period of duration for the stages of larva and pupa that ranged, respectively, between 5 and 10 days and 2 to 3 days.

The high levels of viability, above 60% for larvae maintained in unpolluted waters, as desclorada water and rain water, are supported by BALL & CASTRO-JUNIOR (2008), which compared the lifetime of four samples of populations of a. aegypti collected in the municipalities of Brejo dos Santos, Boqueirão, Brazil and in Paraíba, found Remígio values above 92.0% survival , at a temperature of 26° C with water desclorada. The smallest development periods established for larvae developed in raw sewage, effluent waters of UASB reactor, anaerobic filter and polishing pond (Tabs. III-V) can be due to higher concentration of nutrients, mainly organic matter such as proteins, carbohydrates and lipids, associated with the supplied as food ration. However, it has not been possible to establish the causes of low larval survival in these types of water collections, while one can raise the hypothesis that would be associated with lower surface tension and viscosity of the water. According to EDWARDS (1988), the surface tension of water decreases with increasing temperature and amount of dissolved organic substances and, when reduced to very low levels, can affect the communities that live on the surface. On the other hand viscosity, which is the ability of the water to provide resistance to movement of organisms, decreases with increasing temperature and dissolved salts content and with the lower viscosity, the organisms tend to sink more quickly. This condition may have occurred in the bioassays gifts, since they were developed in conditions of high temperature, approximately 25° C in the water and 26° C in the environment, low levels of dissolved oxygen, high levels of dissolved organic matter (BOD), total solids, ammoniacal nitrogen and total nitrogen (Tabs. I-III), making thus the larvae in staying on the surface of the water to expose the respiratory siphon and increasing the energy expenditure that is associated with a lower rate of breathing, may have contributed to increased larval mortality. It should be emphasized that the above water collections, there was the formation of a float on the surface material, probably as a consequence of these physical and chemical conditions that possibly served as a barrier to the respiratory siphon, prevented the obtaining of atmospheric oxygen by the larva. However, despite this low viability, it was possible the larval development in different water samples, including those with higher pollution degree.

It is noticed that a. aegypti has ability to develop both in environments with high levels of pollution as in raw domestic sewage, where there is a high concentration of organic material with DBO5 of 300 mg 02/L, ammoniacal nitrogen of 52 mg N-NH4+/L and virtually zero dissolved oxygen (020.12 mg/L), as in treated effluents tertiary level with removal of carbonaceous material, pós-tratado in anaerobic filter effluent with BOD5 of 14 mg 02/L, 5.9 mg 02/L of dissolved oxygen and 33.14 mg N-NH4+/L and polishing pond with DBO5 of 55 mg, 4.5 mg 02/L of dissolved oxygen and 5.49 mg N-NH4+/L (Tab.
