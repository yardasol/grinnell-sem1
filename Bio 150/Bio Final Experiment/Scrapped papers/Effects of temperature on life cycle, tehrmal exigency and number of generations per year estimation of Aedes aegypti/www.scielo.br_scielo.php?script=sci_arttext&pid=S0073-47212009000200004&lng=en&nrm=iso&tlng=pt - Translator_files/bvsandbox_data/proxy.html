Effects of temperature on life cycle, thermal requirements and estimates of the number of annual generations of Aedes aegypti (Diptera, Culicidae)

 

Effects of temperature on life cycle, thermal exigency and number of generations per year estimation of Aedes aegypti (Diptera, Culicidae)

 

 

Eduardo B. BallI; Carlos R. MI; Suêide a. o. Scott(I); Lilian a. da SilvaI; Joseph w. dos SantosII

(I) Department of biology, State University of Paraíba, Ganesh Arruda Street s/n, Bodocongó, 58109-753 Campina Grande, PB, Brazil. (ebeserra@uol.com.br; ebarbosa@uepb.edu.br)
II National Research Center of cotton, Embrapa cotton, Rua Osvaldo Cruz 1143, Centenário, 58107 720 PB-Campina Grande, Brazil. (jwsantos@cnpa.embrapa.br)

 

 

SUMMARY

The study aimed to evaluate the effect of temperature on the life cycle of Aedes aegypti (Linnaeus, 1762), determine the thermal requirements for the development and estimate the number of annual generations of the insect in the field. The life cycle of a. aegypti has been studied at temperatures from 18, 22, 26, 28, 32 and 34 ± 2° C and 12 hours of photophase, determining the thermal limits of development (Tb) and the thermal constants (K) and the estimated number of annual generations of the insect in the laboratory and in the field. The temperature conducive to the development of a. aegypti is between 22° C and 32° C, and for longevity and fecundity of adults between 22° C and 28° c. The Tb, K from egg to adult, and the number of annual generations in the field were 11.33; 8.99 and 13.61° C, 192.3; 213.2 and 116.5 degrees-days, and 23; and 30.3 24.6 generations for the populations of a. aegypti of Boqueirão (07° 29 ' 27 '' S, 36° 08 ' 09 ' W), Campina Grande (07° 13 ' 32 '' S, 35° 54 ' 15 '' W) and Remigio (58 ' number 1 ' 06 ' S, 35° 47 ' 29 '' W), respectively.

Keywords: Insecta, Aedini, biology, thermal requirements.

ABSTRACT

The present work aims at estimating, based on a biological study, the thermal requirements to the development and the number of generations per year of Aedes aegypti (Linnaeus, 1762) (Diptera, Culicidae) in field. The life cycle of the a. aegypti populations has been studied at constant temperatures of 18, 22, 26, 28, 32 and 34 ± 2ºc, and 12 hours photophase. The low threshold temperature of development (Tb) and the thermal constant (K) have been determined. The number of generations per year in laboratory and field has also been estimated. The favourable price temperature to the a. aegypti development its between 22ºc and 32° C, and to the longevity and fecundity a. aegypti adult its between 22ºc and 28ºc. The egg to adult basal temperature, thermal constant and the number of generations in field were, in order, 11.33; 8.99 and 13.61° C, 192.3; 213.2 and 116.5 degrees-days, and 23; 30.3 24.6 and generations to a. aegypti populations from Boqueirão (07° 29 ' 27 '' S, 36° 08 ' 09 ' W), Campina Grande (07° 13 ' 32 '' S, 35° 54 ' 15 '' W) and Remigio (58 ' number 1 ' 06 ' S, 35° 47 ' 29 '' W).

Keywords: Insecta, Aedini, biology, thermal requirements.

 

 

Aedes (Stegomyia) aegypti (Linnaeus, 1762) is the most important mosquito transmission of yellow fever and of the four serotypes of the dengue virus (LOZOVEI, 2001; ALDAMA & GARCIA, 2001; FORATTINI, 2002). Is a cosmopolitan insect, well adapted to the urban environment, distributed in tropical and subtropical areas, located between 45 degrees north latitude and 40° south latitude (LOZOVEI, 2001; FORATTINI, 2002).

Aedes aegypti has developed in their evolutionary path a strictly sinantrópico and antropofílico behavior, being recognized among the culicidae as the species more associated with the man (Christmas, 2002). Multiple factors involved in the dispersion of culicidae domiciled. The geographic expansion of the populations of a. aegypti suffers the influence of social and environmental factors, including population density, economic activity and the climate. The influence of climate on the distribution and abundance of insects and the epidemiology of areas of occurrence of diseases transmitted by vectors in several continents and their aggravation had, among others, increasing the temperature of the planet, especially over the last 100 years (GLASSER & GARCIA, 2002).

In the State of São Paulo was verified a strong association between the average temperature of July and the establishment of the populations of a. aegypti, and the speed of occupation of new municipalities was greater the higher the temperature range. In regions with temperatures above 16-18° C in less than 2 years after the establishment of the vector 10% of municipalities were infested, amounting to 90% after five years, as for example the mesoregions of Araçatuba, Presidente Prudente, Ribeirão Preto and São José do Rio Preto, where the July isotherms were above 18° C (GLASSER & GARCIA, 2002). In Nova Iguaçu (Rio de Janeiro), the frequency of a. aegypti was higher in the months of December and February, which were the warmest months, with mean temperatures above 25º and increased precipitation in summer, being the lowest period of occurrence the winter months (HAKEEM & LAWRENCE-DE-OLIVEIRA, 2001). In the State of Paraíba the amplitude of temperature environment-life cycle of a. aegypti is between 22° C and 30° C, and can occur under these conditions up to 20 generations of the insect throughout the year (BALL et al., 2006).

Temperature is an important ecological factor influencing the establishment of insect populations, either directly through its development, or indirectly through their food (SILVEIRA NETO et al., 1976). On the basis of temperature studies that determine the thermal requirements for the development of a. aegypti and representations of the various climatic regions of occurrence of the mosquito through normal weather it is possible to have a better understanding of the population dynamics and predict the number of generations per year, as well as the times of greatest occurrence in the areas of infestation. Thus, it is essential to the development of bioecológicos studies to aid in the understanding of the population dynamics of mosquito to develop predictive models of occurrence of a. aegypti in areas vulnerable to infestations. This research aimed to evaluate the effect of temperature on the life cycle of a. aegypti, thermal requirements for its development and estimate the number of annual generations of the insect in the field.

 

MATERIAL AND METHODS

The creations of the populations of a. aegypti and the laboratory bioassays were conducted in Biological control laboratory in the Center for integrated pest management at the State University of Paraíba, using the first generation of laboratory samples of three vector populations collected in the municipalities of Boqueirão (07° 29 ' 27 '' S, 36° 08 ' 09 ' W), Campina Grande (07° 13 ' 32 '' S , 35° 54 ' 15 '' W) and Remigio (58 ' number 1 ' 06 ' S, 35° 47 ' 29 '' W) (fig. 1). Samples of mosquito populations were obtained from eggs collected in ovitrampas traps installed in intra and peridomestic habitats of randomly chosen households in ten blocks, being installed 50 traps in the neighborhood of Pedegral (Campina Grande) and 30 traps in the Bela vista District in Boqueirão and Ramil.

The reeds of eucatex ovitrampas traps containing eggs of a. aegypti were placed to dry for a period of 48 hours and then wrapped in white plastic trays measuring 40 cm long x 27 wide x 7.5 cm deep with one-third of their capacity filled with desclorada water. After hatching, was offered an ornamental fish feed in proportion of 100 mg/tray, every three days and the trays covered by a fine mesh screen. The pupae were sexed according FORATTINI (2002) and subsequently transferred in 250 ml disposable cups for breeding cages for adults. These cages, built of wood frame and organza fabric measuring 40 cm x 40 cm x 20 cm, received 100 individuals, in the ratio of one male to every female. Adults offered a solution of honey to 20% and females were allowed repast in blood quail, Coturnix japonica (Temminck & Schlegel, 1849) for 30 minutes, three times a week. After the repast was placed in each cage, a disposable Cup to 250 ml with water desclorada with a plastic funnel covered with a filter paper to serve as a substrate for oviposition.

Life cycle of a. aegypti in six constant temperatures. The biological cycle of each population was studied in air-conditioned Chambers governed the 18 ± 2° C, 22 ± 2ºc, 26 ± 2ºc, 28 ± 2° C, 32 ± 2° C and 34 ± 2° C and 12 hours of photophase. For the study of larval development were placed, for each temperature, 5 cups 250 ml polyethylene, containing 10 larvae (F1) of 1st stage of each population, repeated five times. For the larvae offered, daily, 10 mg of fodders for fishes. Twice a day was measured the water temperature of the glass, using a Mercury thermometer with a variation of 0º C to 60º C. The adults were kept in wooden cages telada (20 cm3) containing twenty couples in five repetitions, being offered a solution of honey to 20%, in addition to allow females the repast in 20 minutes during quail blood three times a week. For the egg phase were evaluated the first 20 positions each treatment. These bets were placed in glass Petri dish (15 cm diameter x 2.5 cm deep) with desclorada water, considering how impractical the eggs until the 20th day had not hatched larvae. We evaluated the development period and the viabilities of the phases of egg, larva and pupa, the sex ratio and the longevity and fecundity of adulthood.

The experimental design was completely randomized, with the parsed data in 3 x 6 factorial scheme, three populations and six temperatures, to the stages of egg, larva, pupa and adult of a. aegypti. Biological variables evaluated were submitted to analysis of variance and averages compared by Tukey test (p < 0.05).

Thermal requirements for the development and estimate of the number of annual generations of a. aegypti. Based on the duration of the development of immature and total cycle obtained 18 ± 2° C, 22 ± 2ºc, 26 ± 2ºc, 28 ± 2° C, 32 ± 2° C and 34 ± 2º C lower thermal thresholds were determined (tb) and the thermal constants (K) through the method of the Hyperbola (HADDAD & PARRA, 1984) given by the equation K = y (t-tb) where : K is the thermal constant, y development time (days), t temperature (° C), and tb development threshold temperature. From biological data obtained in the temperature at which the cycle was completed in less time and in function of the thermal constant of the biological cycle, the total degree days available for the normal development and in the areas of thermal study, it was estimated the number of annual generations of the insect, in the laboratory and in the field. The number of annual generations of the insect was estimated based on your requirements, using equation NG = T (tc tb)/K, where T is the time considered in years and tc monthly average temperatures of the study regions, being K and tb the parameters already defined previously.

DISCUSSION

The results showed that populations of a. aegypti from the municipalities of Boqueirão, Campina Grande and Ramil showed distinct patterns of development in relation to temperature and that the development of immature stages, the longevity of adults and the fecundity of females were influenced by the temperatures of 18, 22, 26, 28, 32 and 34 ± 2ºc, coinciding with the results from draft & NAVARRO-SILVA (2002a) for Aedes (s.) albopictus Skuse , 1894. Those authors have verified an inverse relationship between development time and temperature where the lowest periods of duration of the phases of egg, larva and pupa occurred with increasing temperature. For the egg hatching period there has been a reduction of approximately 27 days when the eggs were kept at 25° C and 30° C temperature of 15ºc. These differences, as the pattern of development were also detected for other populations of a. aegypti from the municipalities of Boqueirão, Brejo dos Santos, Campina Grande, Brazil and Ramil, all in the State of Paraíba, where differences about the pattern of development were considered inherent in each population, arising as a result of ecological adaptations to their regions of origin (BALL et al. , 2006).

In this study all temperatures allowed the development of populations of a. aegypti and not been found negative effects on the viabilities of the phases of egg, larva and pupa (although they have influenced the development of immature stages). With the exception of egg viabilities of populations of Boqueirão and Remígio to 18, 22 and 34° C and the pupal stage of the population of Campina Grande to 28 C in all other treatments was observed more than 70%, surpassing the 90% for the entire larval stage of a. aegypti populations of Campina Grande and Ramil.

The effects of temperature on the life cycle of insects vectors have been reported by several authors (COSTA et. Al., 1994; RIBEIRRO et al., 2001; QUIET & NAVARRO-SILVA, 2002a, b, c; BALL et. Al., 2006). Studies on Culex quiquefasciatus Say, 1823, demonstrated that embryonic development occurred at temperatures that fluctuated between 10° C and 34° C, but not to 5° C and 40° C, and the larval development period decreased from 53.7 days at 15° C to 30° C days 7.4 (COSTA et. Al., 1994). For this species was also observed a viability of eggs up 97.9% at temperatures between 15° C and 30° C and a decrease in the period of larval development of 35.7 days 15ºc to 7.5 days to 30ºc (RIBEIRRO et. Al., 2004). However, it should be emphasized that low temperatures are often harmful to the development of insects (e.g. a. albopictus, for which the temperature of 20° C caused 50% mortality of eggs) (QUIET & NAVARRO-SILVA, 2002c). For c. quinquefasciatus and a. aegypti low infestations were recorded as a result of the low temperatures of the State of São Paulo, probably due to lower fertility of females and the increase in the period of development of the immature stages (GLASSER & GARCIA, 2002). In addition, the increased development can result in increased mortality of immature under natural conditions, given the longer exposure to predation, parasitism and disease (TRIPS & SHEMANCHUK, 1970).

As noted for the development of the immature stages of a. aegypti, the temperature had no effect on the adult phase and thermal extremes of 18ºc and 34ºc decreased longevity of adults and the fecundity of females. This was also noted for populations of a. aegypti from the municipalities of Boqueirão, Brejo dos Santos, Campina Grande, Brazil and Ramil (where there has been less longevity of adults and lower fecundity of females when they were subjected to temperatures of 18ºc and 34ºc) (BALL et. Al., 2006) and a. albopictus whose longevity average was 19.4 and 13.6 days for females and males respectively (QUIET & NAVARRO-SILVA , 2002c).

It was found that the thermal limits (tb) of development and their thermal constants (K), expressed in degrees-days (GD), to the stages of egg, larva, pupa and adult to egg, varied according to the stages of development and populations studied, which was also checked for Ophyra aenescens Wiedemann 1830 (Diptera: Muscidae) (RIBEIRRO et al., 2001), a. albopictus (QUIET & NAVARRO-SILVA , 2002b), c. quinquefasciatus (RIBEIRRO et. Al., 2004) and a. aegypti (BALL et. Al., 2006). Such variations are consequences of structural and physiological differences inherent in each stage of the insect's development, which ensures the species the ability to adapt to environmental changes (QUIET & NAVARRO-SILVA, 2002b).

In this research it was found that the population of Remígio presented the highest temperature base for the development of the insect from egg to adult emergence, being the lowest for the population of Brazil. Such a factor was not expected to be these two municipalities located in the same region, Northeastern biogeographical dry hot toned, with normal average thermal 23.3° C (laboratory of meteorology, water resources and Remote Sensing of Paraiba). However, the results obtained are in agreement with studies done with population of a. aegypti collected in Remígio, for which the thermal threshold for development from egg to adult was 13.5° C, which is higher than that observed for other populations as Boqueirão (9.5° C), Brejo dos Santos (8.5° C), Brazil and Campina Grande (3.5° C) (BALL et. al. , 2006).

Based on the development and viability of the phases of egg, larva and pupa and fecundity of adults, it was found that the temperature favourable to vector is above 22° C and under 32 degrees, therefore within the temperature range of the its regions of occurrences. The bases for the development of the various phases of the life cycle of the insect were lower than minimum temperatures of each region. So, considering the average temperature environment of each region where are situated the towns of Boqueirão, Campina Grande and Ramil (approximately 25° C) all localities boast conditions conducive to the development and establishment of the populations of a. aegypti, may submit up to twenty generations throughout the year.
